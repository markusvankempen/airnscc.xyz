Volatile organic compounds also known as (VOCs)

(VOCs) are commonly found in both indoor and outdoor air. They are from a large group of chemicals and can affect the quality of our air. Some (VOCs) give off distinct odours at high levels but most have no smell at all. Some common smells might include; natural smells such as pine sap and oranges. Or more man made smells such as drying paint or vinyl, wood or carpeted flooring. (VOCs) can cause difficulty breathing, irritation in your eyes, nose and throat, loss or coordination, damage to your central nervous system and other organs, and much more. Not every gas included in (VOC) can cause health risks but it’s important for us to monitor it so we can minimize the risks. Some ways that you can minimize your risks is to make sure that you have plenty of ventilation in your house, choose your paint wisely, and read manufactures labels when using household chemicals.   Some common examples of (VOC) gasses include, Benzene, Ethylene, Glycol, Formaldehyde, Methylene Chloride and many more. Activated carbon air purifiers and filters can remove any (VOC) that might be in your house.  According to experts anything from a level of 0.3-0.5 mg/m3 is considered safe.



Fine particulate matter also known as (PM2.5)

(PM2.5) are tiny particles in the air and are a health hazard when levels are high. Some signs of high levels of (PM2.5) may include loss of visibility and hazy looking air.  (PM2.5) might cause; coughing, sneezing, irritation of the eyes, nose and throat. Worsened effects of lung diseases such as asthma, and in bad cases serious heart problems. Things that you can do to reduce your levels of (PM2.5) is to get an air filter, keep your house clean, don’t smoke inside your house, and avoid anything that burns such as a fireplace or candles when possible. (PM2.5) could be any type of particle that is under 2.5 microns in diameter. Some common indoor sources of (PM2.5) include dander, mold, bacteria, chemicals from cleaning products, candles, cooking, sweeping and much more. According to experts PM2. 5 at or below 12 μg/m3 is considered healthy with little to no risk, but anything higher than 35 μg/m3 during a 24-hour period. Puts people at risk of breathing problems.








Temperature and humidity are two factors that can alter the effects of air quality

For example high humidity increases the rate of harmful chemicals in the air, it also causes the damping of the overall quality of the air and causes many different types of bacteria and viruses to thrive. It’s also why people are more likely to be sick if they get soaked in the rain. Temperature works the same way in that higher temperatures lead to less circulated air, and thus the quality of said air decreases over time.  The best way that we can work to eliminate these issues is to lessen or reduce our emissions. So that we can all enjoy safe clean air.
